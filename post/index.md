Five of the most-traded tokenized-stock pools on Solana carry a large share of manufactured volume: fleets of automated wallets that buy and sell the same pool in matched size, returning to flat after every round trip, several of them funded into existence in coordinated chains. What a centralized tape would report as one inflated number, this post takes apart wallet by wallet - naming them, reconstructing how they were funded, and pointing you to each one on-chain.

> **Scope.** The flagged conduct is by pseudonymous, automated wallets. Nothing here indicates that Backed Finance, Kraken, the Solana Foundation, Raydium, or Orca operate these wallets, are aware of them, or benefit from them; xStock pools are permissionless, and a venue or issuer does not control who trades in them. This is a flag on an on-chain trading pattern, not a finding that any person acted with the intent to mislead that wash trading requires in law.

## Summary

1. **A wallet-level detector over the liquid pools of nine xStock tickers flags five.** Scoring the 16 pools with enough swaps (14 xStock pools plus two organic-token controls), the flag is the share of dollar volume from *balanced heavy round-trippers*: wallets that buy and sell the same pool at least five times each, with total buy and sell dollars within 10% of each other. The five flagged pools score 0.36 to 0.80. The a-priori controls, WIF and JUP, score zero, and every other scored pool, including the same xStocks in their other pools, scores at most 0.06. A clean gap, not a continuum. This is a screen of nine pre-selected tickers, not a census of every xStock.
2. **The signature is a wallet that buys and sells in matched size for no net position.** Pool-level "circular volume" does not separate wash from organic: over a short window most active wallets in any pool both buy and sell, so the liquid controls score 74% and 38% circular, as high as some flagged pools. The wallet-level definition separates them completely. The organic controls contain no such wallet; the flagged pools are dominated by a handful. On QQQX the busiest one bought $10,657 and sold $10,695 across 36 buys and 36 sells.
3. **The round trip ends flat and loses to fees.** Buy and sell dollars match to a fraction of a percent, the sells returning 99.3% of what the buys cost, and the shares come back too: netting each wallet's xStock units, the leftover position is a median 0.01% of the units it turned over, 4.6% at worst. It is not cross-pool arbitrage on either test. No flagged wallet buys in one pool and sells in another inside a transaction, though 38 transactions in the snapshot do exactly that, all by other wallets; and none is buy-heavy in one pool while sell-heavy in another, which is what arbitraging two pools minutes apart would leave behind.
4. **The bots run on a robot's clock.** The QQQX wallet's 72 swaps alternate buy, sell with no exception at a roughly fixed cadence, its position sawtoothing between zero and about $300 and returning to flat after each sell. Three TSLAX wallets run the same 4 to 5 second cadence back-to-back. The organic controls hold no such round-tripper to behave this way.
5. **Where the funding can be traced, the wallets are a co-deployed fleet rather than independent traders.** In TSLAX/Orca the five wash bots and the two wallets above them form a seven-wallet creation chain: every wallet after the first had its opening transaction paid for by the one before it, which created its token account and seeded it with about 500 USDT, the six seeds stepping down 4.3 to 4.6 USDT at a time. Three SPYX wallets run the same 7-and-7 buy/sell pattern for near-identical dollars, about $12,220 each; counting only its own transactions, and not the legs of routed swaps, one wallet round-trips SPYX in all three of its pools. (The serial peel chain is shown for TSLAX/Orca; in two more pools the bots share an upstream funder, which a depth-capped trace cannot resolve to a single operator. In the remaining two, QQQX/Raydium and one SPYX/Orca pool, there is no funding link between the bots at all.)
6. **It is the pool, not the token.** QQQX is flagged in one Raydium pool and clean in another; TSLAX is flagged on Orca and clean on Raydium. The contract and the equity behind it do not change between the two pools, only the venue does.
7. **The wallets predate the pools, and the fleet is mixed.** Some have been on-chain since October 2025, before the flagged pools existed, with no xStock activity until recently; others, like the TSLAX peel chain, were created the day of the snapshot, and the funding wallets reach back to December 2024. Aged and same-day alike, with funding older than the pools. That dates the accounts and the money behind them, not the washing itself, which this data cannot place in time.
8. **Scale, stated carefully.** In the measured windows the bots round-tripped $467,000 of buy-and-sell that nets to nothing, a hard floor, directly observed. A 24-hour figure is an extrapolation, and the two natural methods disagree by about threefold across the five pools ($32M to $102M per day) and by far more on an individual pool: widest on QQQX, where the share method implies $26M against under $1M from that pool's own observed bot rate. The floor and the per-pool shares are the claims to rely on. Followed back through their on-chain history in the three xStocks the lifetime collector recognises (SPYX, TSLAX, QQQX), these same wallets have matched $5.6M across 2,836 swaps - a floor, since any other xStock they touched is not counted.
9. **Read it as a flag on behaviour, not a verdict.** Solana addresses are pseudonymous. This characterises a pattern of automated, self-cancelling trading and its funding structure, not the identity or intent of any person. Every wallet and transaction below is checkable on a public explorer, and the active wallet set rotates over time.

## The market: xStocks, on-chain on Solana

[xStocks](https://xstocks.com) are tokenized US equities and ETFs issued by Backed, which buys the share through a broker, holds it with a regulated custodian and mints one SPL token against it, so supply tracks the shares one for one ([Solana Foundation case study](https://solana.com/news/case-study-xstocks)). They [went live on 30 June 2025](https://backed.fi/news-updates/xstocks-are-going-live-tokenized-stocks-for-the-defi-era) with Kraken and Bybit as launch partners, and trade against USDC, USDT and SOL on Raydium, Orca and Meteora, with Jupiter routing quotes. A companion study found their centralized-exchange markets carry a [pervasive wash-trading pattern on Gate](https://github.com/mkzung/gate-xstocks-wash-analysis); the question here is whether the same kind of pattern appears in the tokens' native, fully transparent venue. It does - and on-chain the analysis can do what no centralized tape allows: name the wallets, reconstruct their funding, and point the reader to each account on-chain.

The macro shape is a hint: several xStock pools turn over their entire liquidity tens to a thousand times a day (QQQX: $33.7M of volume on a $20.7k pool, 1,624x) where an organic token like WIF turns over a fraction of its liquidity. This article resolves that hint pool by pool, then wallet by wallet.

## A screen of the liquid xStock pools

The universe is a pre-selection, and it bounds everything that follows. A Dexscreener pull over nine xStock tickers - SPYX, TSLAX, NVDAX, CRCLX, QQQX, MSTRX, COINX, METAX, GOOGLX - returns 32 pools, committed as `data/universe.json` in the [companion repository](https://github.com/mkzung/solana-xstocks-wash-analysis) - every file path in this post is in that repo, not in this post's directory. That list was pulled from Dexscreener once and committed, not re-queried by the pipeline, and xStocks outside it are not in the universe at all - so nothing here is a statement about the full xStock lineup. The collector fetches a tape only for the pools with at least $50,000 of reported 24-hour volume, which leaves 14; all 14 then clear the second filter, at least 150 swaps in the fetched tape, so in practice the volume floor does all of the excluding. Two organic controls run through the identical pipeline, giving the 16 scored pools. The other 18 xStock pools are unscored rather than clean, and some are busy: NVDAX/Orca [`6R4r93V5`](https://solscan.io/account/6R4r93V5fcMzc13CL2enEepDSYcr4Qx3ptZBDwudTXCo) turned over 1,365 swaps in the same 24 hours on $27k of volume, and two more sit just under the line ($46.7k on TSLAX/Raydium, $40.4k on QQQX/Raydium). The floor is a collection cutoff, not a calibrated threshold, and it is the reason "five of sixteen" counts the scored pools rather than every pool these tokens trade in. The pool-level score is the share of dollar volume transacted by wash-bot wallets, where a wash bot is defined purely at the wallet level:

> a wallet that buys and sells the same pool at least five times each (`min(buys, sells) >= 5`), and whose total buy and sell dollars land within 10% of each other (`min/max >= 0.90`).

The definition is deliberately set at the wallet level rather than the pool level. A pool-level circularity measure - the share of a pool's volume done by wallets that both buy and sell, which is a construction of this post rather than one of the DN metrics - does not separate wash from organic: over a short window most active wallets in any liquid pool trade both ways, so WIF and JUP score 74% and 38% circular, as high as the flagged pools. The wallet-level definition separates the organic controls completely: WIF and JUP contain no such wallet at all. It isolates an actor neither control contains, one that market-takes both directions in matched size, repeatedly, ending flat. That is a statement about the two controls measured here, not about real markets in general. Among the non-flagged xStock pools the separation is sharp rather than absolute - one wallet sits on the line at 0.91, and the sub-window caveat below applies.

**Calibration is not circular.** The 0.20 flag sits above the two a-priori organic controls, WIF and JUP, both of which score zero and were chosen before anything was scored. It is not defined by turnover, which is itself a wash signal, so the clean xStock pools are a result: the same QQQX and TSLAX that flag in one pool fall below 0.06 in their other pools under the same detector.

The five flagged pools are TSLAX/Orca (0.80, 5 bots), QQQX/Raydium (0.78, 2 bots), SPYX/Raydium (0.43, 5 bots), and two SPYX/Orca pools, [`gef4pD5g`](https://solscan.io/account/gef4pD5g6GJSjX7hfXKUfhCVpE89BzX9TXnXJNW6kSt) at 0.41 and [`6m6UoVxn`](https://solscan.io/account/6m6UoVxnmGePnHQ8V6gGrgDba5p1v7MCpM9Ji1LBBVPJ) at 0.36 (3 bots each), which are also the five highest-volume xStock pools in the universe. Those counts are per pool and add up to eighteen; two wallets trade in more than one pool, so the number of distinct wallets is fourteen.

Two honesty notes the reader should weigh:

- **The 0.90 cut fails toward missing wash, not inventing it.** Re-scoring every pool across `min_rt` in {3, 5, 8, 10} and balance in {0.80, 0.90, 0.95}, judged against the only ground truth here - the a-priori controls, fixed before anything was scored: at 0.90 neither WIF nor JUP is flagged, but loosen the balance to 0.80 and WIF crosses the flag at every `min_rt`, its score jumping from 0 to 0.73. A token picked in advance as organic, then flagged, is a real false positive; a merely non-flagged xStock pool crossing would not count as one, since "non-flagged" is the same detector's own output, not an independent label. Tightening to 0.95 flags nothing new; it only thins the true set - four of the five flagged pools survive at the detector's default of five buys and five sells, three at the strictest count tested. So the cut errs one way: below 0.90 it starts catching organic two-sided volume, above it only loses genuine pools. It lands at 0.90 because the balances are bimodal: 18 of 33 heavy round-trippers in the flagged pools sit at 0.90 or above, against 1 of 22 in the rest, so the line falls in the empty gap between the two clumps.
- **The snapshot is a window, not a day, and the share is window-sensitive.** GeckoTerminal returns the most recent ~300 swaps per pool, which spans 13 to 79 minutes in the busy pools and many hours in the quiet ones. Wash share therefore moves with window length. Slice a quiet pool to the shortest flagged window, about 14 minutes, and one wallet round-tripping inside it can clear the flag; four of the non-flagged pools do exactly that. The share is an in-window intensity, not a daily rate, and one short window is not by itself proof. What separates the flagged pools is a fleet sustained across the whole snapshot: every flagged pool carries two to five balanced bots over its full ~300 swaps, while no non-flagged pool carries more than one. Two further properties hold on subsets, and they are different subsets: three of the five are washing again six hours later (`persistence.py`), and in three of the five the bots trace to shared funding. Only TSLAX/Orca does both. Neither is a blanket property of the fleet, and they are not the same three pools. That count does not rest on routing artefacts either: strike out every wallet that reaches a pool only as the leg of a routed swap, and each flagged pool still carries at least two bots trading it in transactions of their own. The dollar extrapolation in "The scale" is treated accordingly.

In the flagged pools (orange) they pile up at near-perfect balance; in every other pool (green) they are directional and spread below the cut. The 0.90 threshold falls in the gap between the two modes." loading="lazy" >}}

The cut is not tuned, but the flagged-against-non-flagged contrast is not the evidence for it. Of the 22 heavy round-trippers in the non-flagged pools and the controls, one reaches the wash-bot balance: a lone borderline 0.91, in an xStock pool, not a control. The 18 of 33 in the flagged pools sit pressed against 1.0, median 0.94 against 0.66. That gap is worth reporting, but it cannot be used as proof of anything: a pool is flagged precisely because balanced round-trippers move its volume, so flagged pools contain such wallets by construction. Counting them and finding more in the flagged pools measures the selection, not the market. It is the same circularity that rules out a p-value here, and dropping the p-value while keeping the inference would be the same mistake with the number filed off. What the cut does rest on is the a-priori controls, chosen before anything was scored. In the sweep above, WIF crosses the flag at 0.80 but not at 0.90, so the cut is loose enough to admit a token known to be organic only once it is pushed below 0.90; and across the full snapshot WIF and JUP carry three heavy round-trippers between them, not one of them balanced. Three wallets is a thin comparison and is not a test either. The claim that these bots are unlike organic traders rests on the signals the detector never looks at: the fixed cadence, the clip sizes matched within a few dollars, the funding chains, and the near-zero token flatness below. Turnover, a signal the detector does not use, corroborates the thin pools strongly (QQQX 1,624x) but only weakly the deep ones (SPYX/Raydium, a $1.67M pool at 56x), where the wallet structure carries the flag.

**The pattern recurs, and the wallets do not.** Re-sampled once about six hours later, not one wash bot reappears: across the two snapshots wallet overlap is zero. Three of the five pools are again actively washed (TSLAX 75%, QQQX 69%, the SPYX/Orca pool [`gef4pD5g`](https://solscan.io/account/gef4pD5g6GJSjX7hfXKUfhCVpE89BzX9TXnXJNW6kSt) 41%); the other two sit below the flag at that moment. This is one before-and-after comparison, not a time series, so it shows complete turnover across this interval rather than a proven general cadence. The activity comes in bursts by a rotating set of wallets, so a single window catches some pools mid-burst and others between - which is why any named wallet is point-in-time. `persistence.py` reproduces the comparison.

## The signature: matched buys and sells

Plot every wallet by the dollars it bought against the dollars it sold. A wallet that buys and sells in matched size sits on the diagonal; a directional trader sits off it.

{{< figure src="signature.png" alt="scatter of USD bought versus USD sold per wallet, wash bots on the diagonal in the flagged pools, organic wallets scattered" caption="USD bought versus USD sold per wallet. Left: the flagged xStock pools, where the wash bots (orange) sit on the buy=sell diagonal at $3k to $33k. Right: an organic control (WIF/JUP), where the busiest wallets are directional or one-sided, off the diagonal." loading="lazy" >}}

In the flagged pools the heavy wallets line up on the diagonal at five figures; in the organic control the busiest wallets sit off it, many pure buyers or pure sellers on the axes. On QQQX/Raydium the busiest wallet, [`C6FyA84D...`](https://solscan.io/account/C6FyA84D6JLtkSSyq45gcLFi67P3q61FhGohEFYd9rvF), bought $10,657 and sold $10,695; on TSLAX/Orca four wallets each bought and sold near $10,100, their own buy and sell within a fraction of a percent. A wallet that buys and sells the same amount, in the same pool, ends essentially flat and captures no spread.

This is the place to rule out the benign readings, one at a time:

- **No cross-pool arbitrage signature, on the two tests these tapes allow.** An arbitrageur leaves a mark, and these wallets leave neither version of it in the sampled tapes. Inside a transaction, the snapshot holds 38 that buy in one pool and sell in another, and not one is a flagged wallet's: of the bots' 60 multi-pool transactions, every leg is on the same side. Across transactions, arbitraging two pools minutes apart still ends buy-heavy in one and sell-heavy in the other, and no flagged wallet does that. Most are balanced in every pool they touch, and the only one that leans, `33X9awze`, is sell-heavy in every SPYX pool it trades in its own transactions, which is inventory being run down, not two pools being played against each other. Nor does the round trip pay: sells return about 99.3% of what the buys cost. An offsetting leg on a venue this data does not cover still cannot be excluded.
- **Routed, but the wallet's own trade.** Each flagged address is a plain keypair owned by the System Program, not a router executing other people's flow. Their swaps are often split by an aggregator across several pools inside one signed transaction: 35% of the flagged wallets' volume sits in transactions that touch more than one pool, and for [`6G11Z8xR`](https://solscan.io/account/6G11Z8xR1auD4mbgkh9minP9fQycKWdQcAJ7uxH8Smoz) it is all of it, one $5,000 buy of SPYX split across three pools at once. A pool tape therefore shows a leg of a swap, not the swap. That is an execution detail, not a change of actor: the wallet signs and pays, and the round trip still nets flat.
- **Not plain market-making, though off-venue compensation is not excluded.** A market maker is paid the spread; these wallets pay it, giving back 99.3% of what they put in, and they take both sides at the same price rather than quoting around it. That rules out being paid the spread on these legs, not a maker rebate or a hedge earned somewhere this data does not see; the analysis does not measure liquidity provision. What it does show is that the pattern rests on no single balanced wallet, which an active venue can produce, but on coordinated fleets: a TSLAX/Orca peel chain each funded into existence by the last, three SPYX wallets running the same 7-and-7 buy/sell counts for near-identical dollars, all on a near-perfect cadence.
- **Not a lookalike token.** Each flagged pool's base mint matches Backed's official xStock ([Solana Foundation](https://solana.com/news/case-study-xstocks)), trading at the shares' real prices (TSLAX near $401, SPYX near $750) - what is manufactured is volume, not a price.

What remains is a wallet whose only product is volume: small, similar-sized swaps (around 0.4 QQQX, about $300 each) bought and sold in equal number, so the token position returns to flat and the dollar position nearly so.

## The mechanism: a wallet that ends every round trip flat

The wallet [`C6FyA84D...`](https://solscan.io/account/C6FyA84D6JLtkSSyq45gcLFi67P3q61FhGohEFYd9rvF) makes 72 swaps that alternate buy, sell, buy, sell with no exception, a median 17 seconds apart; its running position rises one clip on each buy and falls back on the next sell, a sawtooth between zero and about $300 that returns to flat after every pair. Across the flagged pools each pool's busiest bot alternates 82% to 100% of the time (the QQQX leader perfectly), and in TSLAX/Orca three wallets run the same 4 to 5 second cadence back-to-back, one finishing as the next starts. Near-perfect alternation at a fixed cadence is a programmatic signature: a loop running `buy; sell; buy; sell`. The organic controls contain no balanced heavy round-tripper at all, so none exhibits this cadence - `temporal.py` runs the same cadence routine over WIF and JUP and returns an empty result for both.

## The coordination: a fleet built from one funding chain

Following each flagged wallet back to its first-ever transaction, through the free Solana RPC, shows how the fleet was built.

{{< figure src="funding.png" alt="seven wallets in a line: two funders in grey, then the five TSLAX wash bots in orange, every wallet after the first seeded by the one before it with a slightly smaller USDT amount" caption="The TSLAX/Orca fleet as a creation chain. Every wallet after the first had its opening transaction paid for by the one before it, which created its token account and seeded it with about 500 USDT. The six seeds fall in steps of 4.3 to 4.6 USDT (526.53, 522.22, 517.67, 513.34, 508.83, 504.48), the signature of automated sequential deployment." loading="lazy" >}}

In the TSLAX/Orca pool the five wash bots, plus the two wallets above them, form a single seven-wallet chain: [`H9c7D19P`](https://solscan.io/account/H9c7D19PycFbYRiZMEBN4UvV9JyCWmQovqruNaG8K11i) seeded [`85zuUQ5w`](https://solscan.io/account/85zuUQ5wHER33mpqxezdaUNjnWfL6fhh5Z7wNnaURTjS) with 526.53 USDT, which seeded [`8gw6JyEW`](https://solscan.io/account/8gw6JyEW1PGLzehpNeNU4jzb2iVtjet1o7YHQPxM1wmH) with 522.22, which seeded [`FMMs8SGx`](https://solscan.io/account/FMMs8SGxukt4DRy1wKGyjCcovqj2xci16doZDPqc33eC) with 517.67, which seeded [`AtzmNv2w`](https://solscan.io/account/AtzmNv2ww5iQRT5XytY43SKvTTeFPL3LdrFPeCiJDFAv) with 513.34, which seeded [`vwbEYDGU`](https://solscan.io/account/vwbEYDGUamnFvhr9fq6RQnXK8Pn76okVw1kwgFPpt9Z) with 508.83, which seeded [`2eTkWQyt`](https://solscan.io/account/2eTkWQyt9iGuXP5XUayXRdZuvLoHzDf48ZDjY8EWQUiF) with 504.48. Every wallet below the top of the chain had its first transaction *signed and paid for* by its predecessor, which created its USDT token account and funded it in the same transaction. The trace walks six levels up and stops, which is where `H9c7D19P` sits: it may well have a funder of its own, but this data does not reach it. Fresh wallets created in sequence, each seeded a near-constant step below the last, is the structure of an automated deployment, not of independent people choosing to trade the same pool. Two further patterns confirm coordination within the fleet:

- **Matching parameters.** In SPYX/Raydium three distinct wallets ([`78jkE1hP`](https://solscan.io/account/78jkE1hPATuzEnAY6ENpfuLf9DZYtXS5iqnmYAeFpFNY), [`3T1YdUjo`](https://solscan.io/account/3T1YdUjo2edVC1oJ6MK7HvKic2JKGs53GC4HuEV6U7rv), [`B2ES7Uzw`](https://solscan.io/account/B2ES7UzwvJTw2A7dX2WpvqQ7xxB58AkSbSp4WbE2eCqL)) each ran exactly 7 buys and 7 sells for about $12,220, their totals within about $20 of one another ($12,228, $12,218, $12,213 bought); in TSLAX/Orca three wallets each ran 21 buys and 21 sells near $10,100 and a fourth ran 20 and 20.
- **Shared operation across pools.** Counted so that one routed swap landing in three pools cannot masquerade as three pools' worth of activity. On that stricter count [`HpcHy6dN`](https://solscan.io/account/HpcHy6dNYRotVim8HPpZmsfnekviyv1sRcX1C533SYky) round-trips SPYX in all three of its pools, the Raydium one and both Orca ones, balanced in each, in transactions of its own. [`33X9awze`](https://solscan.io/account/33X9awzeff9PbKBpQYaf1ZJTp8Ksxv8Fne3WxajLpwM7) also trades three of them in its own transactions - SPYX/Raydium and both SPYX/Orca pools - but runs sell-heavy rather than balanced, so it reads as inventory disposal across the SPYX pools rather than a clean round trip, the weaker case. `6G11Z8xR` is not a case at all: every pool it appears in, it reached only as a leg of a routed swap.

The funding trace was depth-capped, so this analysis does not claim a single operator across all five pools, nor an exchange of origin. The strict serial peel chain is demonstrated for TSLAX/Orca; several other bots - including the largest, [`HpcHy6dN`](https://solscan.io/account/HpcHy6dNYRotVim8HPpZmsfnekviyv1sRcX1C533SYky) - trace to shared upstream funders, which a depth-capped trace cannot distinguish from a common exchange or bridge. What it does show, transaction by transaction, is a funding link between the bots in three of the five flagged pools: the TSLAX/Orca peel chain, where each wallet paid for the next; and SPYX/Raydium and one SPYX/Orca pool, where the bots do not fund each other but trace back to a common funder (`A77HErqt` and `F7p3dFrj` respectively). In the other two, QQQX/Raydium and the second SPYX/Orca pool, the bots trace to different funders with no shared ancestor at any depth the trace reaches. There, what the wallets share is the behaviour - matched size, robotic cadence, no position - and not the money. Coordination is a claim about the pools where the trace supports it, not a blanket one. What does hold across all five, and without leaning on any threshold or control comparison, is the wallet-level behaviour: matched size, robotic cadence, no position at the end. The funding link is what makes three of the pools harder to explain away.

## It is the pool, not the token

The same tokens trade cleanly in their other pools. QQQX is flagged in its Raydium pool [`EibwWLHy`](https://solscan.io/account/EibwWLHyuFeqgU6fvi6g5FtDLF9kQsQL5HQXvcJNkDN8) (78% wash share) yet clean in a second Raydium pool of the identical mint (0 bots); TSLAX is flagged on Orca and clean on Raydium (score 0.06, below the flag). The token, its custodied share, and its contract are the same; only the pool differs. Whatever drives the behaviour tracks the individual pool. It is not a property of the token, and not of the venue either: the clean QQQX pool sits on Raydium, the same venue as the flagged one.

## The scale

{{< figure src="manufactured.png" alt="measured in-window wash share per flagged pool with the matched-USD floor labelled" caption="The measured self-cancelling share of volume in each flagged pool's snapshot window (36% to 80%), with the matched-USD floor and window length labelled. The hard floor across all five is $467k of directly-observed matched volume; the 24h dollar figure is a wide extrapolation, discussed in the text." loading="lazy" >}}

Only one of the two figures is a bound. The hard floor is what the bots round-tripped inside the measured windows: $467,000 of matched buy-and-sell that nets to nothing, directly observed. A 24-hour figure assumes the snapshot behaviour persists, which the re-sample six hours later already contradicts, and the two natural extrapolations disagree sharply. Each pool's wash share times its reported 24h volume gives about $102M a day; each pool's observed bot rate over 24 hours gives about $32M. The gap is widest where the window is shortest: on QQQX the share method implies $26M against under $1M from its own snapshot rate, so that extrapolation is unreliable. Take $32M to $102M per day as the distance between two guesses, not as a range that brackets the truth, and rely on the floor and the per-pool shares.

**The snapshot floor is a fraction of the lifetime total.** Following the fourteen wallets back through their history in the three xStocks the collector recognises - SPYX, TSLAX and QQQX, the symbols that survive the volume floor and so have a known mint - via Helius, classifying each transaction by net token-balance change, shows the snapshot caught a sliver. Even limited to those three symbols they have round-tripped $5.6M of matched stablecoin in 2,836 swaps, more than ten times the in-window floor; any activity in an xStock outside those three is not counted, so this is itself a floor. [`HpcHy6dN`](https://solscan.io/account/HpcHy6dNYRotVim8HPpZmsfnekviyv1sRcX1C533SYky) alone cycled $2.9M across 912 swaps in QQQX, SPYX and TSLAX. That total matches each buy against a sale of the same xStock, so a purchase of one is never cancelled against a sale of another; computed the loose way, across a wallet's symbols at once, it comes to the same $5,579,149 exactly, because these wallets are balanced in every symbol they touch. The washing is not continuous: each wallet concentrates into a span of days, the widest just over four days, the same rotating, short-burst behaviour the persistence re-sample caught. The reconstruction also shows the per-pool tape is conservative, the true on-chain net running about 1.3x what the tape records, so the floor understates the wash if anything.

The fourteen wallets cycled $5.6M across those three symbols; the snapshot caught a sliver." loading="lazy" >}}

What it cost them is not read off a fee schedule, and does not need to be: the round trip's own loss is the bill. The flagged wallets put $236,000 into these pools inside the measured windows and got $234,600 back, the 99.3% above, so the exercise cost about $1,550, pool fees and all. Against the $467,000 of volume it produced, manufacturing a dollar of reported volume cost them about a third of a cent. Solana's [base fee](https://solana.com/docs/core/fees), 5,000 lamports a signature, is negligible beside even that. The committed analysis does not price each pool's fee tier or its priority fees, so no finer breakdown is offered.

For context, the baselines for fabricated volume are large: an oft-cited study puts more than 70% of volume on unregulated venues as non-economic ([Cong et al., *Crypto Wash Trading*, Management Science 2023](https://www.nber.org/papers/w30783)), and Chainalysis - keying, like this detector, on matched buy/sell and multi-address funding clusters - attributed $2.57B of suspected wash trading to 2024 ([Chainalysis 2025](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/crypto-market-manipulation-wash-trading-pump-and-dump-2025/)). The DN wiki has flagged this marker family on centralized venues before ([Huobi 2023](https://github.com/1712n/dn-institute/tree/main/content/research/market-health/posts/2023-08-14-huobi), [Senso 2021](https://github.com/1712n/dn-institute/tree/main/content/research/market-health/posts/2021-01-05-Senso), a companion [Bybit study](https://github.com/1712n/dn-institute/tree/main/content/research/market-health/posts/2026-06-13-bybit)); prior academic work named wash wallets on DEXs generally ([Victor and Weintraud](https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.07001)). The contribution here carries that wallet-level, funding-chain tracing into the tokenized-equity asset class.

## Why volume would be manufactured here

The detector flags behaviour; motive is a separate question, and this data supports several explanations and establishes none. The general incentives for adding on-chain volume are climbing the volume-ranked listings on DEX aggregators (DEX Screener, GeckoTerminal, Jupiter), where visibility follows reported volume, and making a young product look liquid, against a backdrop where xStocks' on-chain liquidity has been reported as [thin and weekend-fragile](https://blockeden.xyz/blog/2025/10/30/xstocks/). A points or liquidity-mining program is the other obvious candidate, and nothing here tests it: this analysis does not measure whether these wallets provide liquidity, and no xStocks-issuer program paying per unit of volume is documented. Motive is left open.

## Why it matters

Reported volume is the number people act on - a retail user choosing where to buy QQQX, a DEX aggregator that orders pools by their reported 24h volume, the product's own promise of deep, around-the-clock liquidity. When a pool shows tens of millions in daily volume and most of it, in the windows measured here, is a handful of wallets cycling stablecoins for no position, that signal is corrupted: the market looks more liquid than it is, and a real trader routed into it acts on a fabricated figure. The point cuts both ways for tokenized equities. Their pitch is transparency - every trade on a public ledger - and that same transparency is what lets the manufactured volume be isolated, counted, and named here, where a centralized order book would show only the inflated total. For a young real-world-asset category courting institutions and regulators, the very metrics used to judge its legitimacy, volume and liquidity, are the ones being inflated.

## How to read this

This is a flag on a pattern, not an accusation against a person. Solana addresses are pseudonymous, and where the funding trace shows coordination it still stops short of identifying who controls the wallets. The US Commodity Exchange Act defines wash trading as entering transactions with no change in beneficial ownership to create a misleading appearance of activity ([CFTC](https://www.cftc.gov/LearnAndProtect/EducationCenter/CFTCGlossary/glossary_wxyz.html)); what is shown here is narrower than that test: each leg trades against the pool rather than against the same owner, so the round trip establishes that the wallet ends with no net position, not that beneficial ownership never changed. Nor is the intent to mislead, the other element the law requires, established here. The regulatory status of tokenized equities is [contested](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/01/29/sec-clarifies-rules-for-tokenized-stocks-tightening-scrutiny-on-synthetic-equity), and xStocks are marketed as unavailable to US persons. None of that is adjudicated here. What is shown is that five Solana xStock pools carry a large share of volume that, by its own on-chain record, is bought and sold in matched size by automated wallets that end with no position, and that in three of those five pools the wallets are tied together by funding, in one case each paying for the next. The named wallets are the active set as of the snapshot and the fleet rotates members over time; but unlike a centralized tape, every statement here is independently checkable - paste any wallet below into [solscan.io](https://solscan.io) and read the manufactured volume off the chain yourself.

## Methodology and reproducibility

The companion repository, [github.com/mkzung/solana-xstocks-wash-analysis](https://github.com/mkzung/solana-xstocks-wash-analysis), reproduces every figure and number from a committed snapshot, with CI that reruns the full pipeline. Every file named here - `data/universe.json`, `analysis/verify.py`, the rest - lives in that repository. The analysis is pinned at commit [`82f58b0`](https://github.com/mkzung/solana-xstocks-wash-analysis/tree/82f58b07db3a8dd42c88e8d8aa06bcf1856e0755). Check that commit out and run the pipeline; `verify.py` recomputes every headline number, and the analysis modules before it regenerate the JSON and figures:

```bash
git clone https://github.com/mkzung/solana-xstocks-wash-analysis
cd solana-xstocks-wash-analysis
git checkout 82f58b07db3a8dd42c88e8d8aa06bcf1856e0755
pip install -r requirements.txt
for m in screen cluster temporal persistence lifetime named_wallets figures; do
  python analysis/$m.py       # rebuild the JSON + PNGs from the committed snapshot
done
python analysis/verify.py     # then independently recompute every headline number
```

[CI](https://github.com/mkzung/solana-xstocks-wash-analysis/actions/workflows/test.yml) runs the same chain on every push. The committed snapshot makes the run deterministic and key-less.

- **Data, free.** Pool universe and turnover from Dexscreener, pulled once and committed as `data/universe.json` rather than re-queried by the pipeline; tx-level swaps (wallet, hash, side, USD) and OHLCV from GeckoTerminal (most-recent ~300 swaps per pool, no deep pagination); funding traces from the public Solana RPC - all key-less. The fourteen bots' lifetime histories in the three recognised xStock symbols (SPYX, TSLAX, QQQX; used only for the lifetime totals in "The scale") come from the Helius enhanced-transactions API (free tier), classified by each wallet's net token-balance change and committed to the repo so the totals recompute without a key. The classifier keys on the mints that survive the volume floor, so any activity in a lower-volume xStock is not counted, and the lifetime totals are floors. The snapshot (2026-06-21) and a second sample taken six hours later (for the persistence check) are both committed, so the analysis is deterministic. The funding trace walks each wallet to its earliest retrievable transaction; for the published chains every history fits in one page, so the origin is the true first transaction.
- **Detector and metric mapping** (`metrics_lib.py`). The wash-bot rule takes the DN [buy/sell metrics](https://dn.institute/market-health/docs/market-health-metrics/) from the pool level down to the wallet level. Two of the DN metrics map onto it directly. The count condition comes from `buysellratio`, which the DN docs define on the *number* of trades: a wallet that only ever buys, or only ever sells, sits at 0 or 1 and can never be a bot, so the rule asks for at least five trades on each side. The balance condition is `buysellratioabs`, the *volume-weighted* one, buy volume over total volume: a wash bot sits on the even split. The 10% band used here is that metric between 0.47 and 0.53. The cadence and alternation shown later are not a DN metric: the DN `timeoftrade` is a second-of-minute histogram over a pool, a different measurement, so no mapping to it is claimed - the fixed inter-swap spacing and near-perfect buy/sell alternation are a wallet-level construction of this post, like the circularity figure. First-digit (`firstdigitdist`/`benfordlawtest`) is not used either: at the wallet level the signal is the matched two-sided structure, not the trade-size distribution. One thing this post does not do is reuse `volumedist`, which the DN docs define as the distribution of trading volumes: the pool-level circularity figure quoted above (the share of volume from wallets that both buy and sell, 74% on WIF and 38% on JUP) is a measure built for this post, not that metric, and it is reported precisely because it fails to separate wash from organic.
- **Pipeline.** Network collectors run once to build the committed snapshot: `fetch_raw.py` (reads the committed `data/universe.json`, the Dexscreener pull, and fetches the tapes from GeckoTerminal; it applies the $50,000 volume floor before fetching anything), `fund_trace.py`/`trace_tree.py` (funding origins via Solana RPC), `wallet_history.py` (each bot's full Helius history), `owner_check.py` (the System-Program-keypair check, committed for offline re-check). The deterministic analysis, which CI reruns over the committed data on every push: `screen.py` (venue screen, controls, sub-window confound check), `cluster.py` (named bots, creation chains, sibling fleets, and the routing measurements in `routing.json`), `temporal.py` (cadence, floor, both 24h extrapolations), `persistence.py`, `lifetime.py`, `named_wallets.py`, `figures.py`, and `verify.py` - an independent recomputation of every headline claim from the committed data - plus a `pytest` suite.
- **Limitations**, stated plainly. The trade snapshot is the most-recent ~300 swaps per pool (a sub-hour window in the busy pools), so wash shares are in-window intensities and the daily dollar figure is the labelled extrapolation, not a measurement. A precise onset cannot be dated: the free OHLCV history is too sparse for a per-pool onset curve, and a wallet's age is not its wash onset (the aged wallets show unrelated activity for months first); the wallet dates establish only that the accounts and the money that created them are older than the pools, not when the washing began. The funding trace is depth-capped, establishing co-deployment in three of the five pools - a serial peel chain for TSLAX/Orca, a shared upstream funder in SPYX/Raydium and one SPYX/Orca pool - and no funding link in the other two, and never a single operator across all five. A pool tape also records legs of swaps, not whole swaps: an aggregator can split one signed transaction across several pools, which covers 35% of the flagged wallets' volume here. That is measured rather than assumed, and it is why a wallet's presence in several pools is only counted when it gets there in transactions of its own. The five flagged pools are also the highest-volume in the universe, and only pools with at least 150 snapshot swaps are scored, so "five of sixteen" is five of the sixteen scored pools, not a census; the low-volume tail is unscored, not shown clean.
